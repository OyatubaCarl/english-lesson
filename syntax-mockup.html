Lesson H60 — 核兵器と国際協力

On August 6, 1945, the world's first atomic bomb was dropped on Hiroshima. Three days later, a second one was dropped on Nagasaki. In both cities, over 200,000 people lost their lives, either at once or in the months that followed.

After the war, the world entered the age of nuclear weapon. The United States and the Soviet Union raced to develop nuclear arms, and the conflict between the two camps was called the Cold War. The threat of conflict reached every corner of the world.

A world frightened by the danger of nuclear war signed the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, the NPT, in 1968. It is an international treaty that tries to prevent more countries from holding nuclear weapons.

But even after the Cold War ended, conflict in the world has not vanished. Regional wars continue, and the number of refugee keeps rising. Syria, Ukraine, Sudan, Myanmar. Many victim have been driven from their homes.

The United Nations has provided a place for negotiation and mediation to prevent war. But when the permanent members of the Security Council disagree, the UN becomes powerless. The limit of diplomacy remains a great challenge.

Peace is not simply the absence of war. True peace is born only when there is the will to respect one another, to speak with one another, and to seek reconciliation. To pass on the memory of Hiroshima and Nagasaki across generations is our responsibility for the future.
